What problem are we solving with the idea?            

Low and medium-quality sheep wool and feathers are a wasteful byproduct of production in farming. These not fully utilized byproducts mainly consist of keratin, the main structural protein found in hair, nails, and feathers. Keratin has been used in cosmetics and medicine for hair, nail, skin renewal, and improvement, but recently great interest has been shown in the use of keratin in biomedicinal applications and biomaterials.
 Despite many studies in this field and attempts to improve keratin yield, only inefficient and complicated keratin extractions methods exist. This slows down the development of keratin applications in these new fields of science. Moreover, these complicated and inefficient keratin extraction methods require specific and expensive equipment or toxic reagents that are harmful to the environment and human health.                     

What is our solution?

The development of a new keratin extraction method that has low installation and processing costs, is an easy adaptative to raw materials, and uses only reagents that are in line with the principles of green chemistry.  

What are the uses for keratin?

Keratin is widely used in cosmetics in different hair products, for its moisturizing properties. It may also be possible to use the harvested proteins in the form of keratin bio-fibers or keratin-based products. In combination with electrospinning, for example, it would be possible to a new type of bio-fibers that could replace synthetic petroleum-based fibers in the textile industry.

But the use cases for keratin do not end there. In recent years, natural-fiber-reinforced polymer composites have also attracted a great deal of interest in the production of more environmentally-friendly replacements for glass or carbon fibers in other composite materials.
